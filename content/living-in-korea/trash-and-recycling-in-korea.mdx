# Trash and Recycling in Korea

Korea takes waste sorting seriously, and the rules surprise many newcomers. Get it wrong and you can face **fines**; get it right and it quickly becomes second nature. Here's the practical breakdown.

## The Three Streams You Must Separate

Korea splits household waste into three main streams:

1. **General waste (일반쓰레기)** — anything that can't be recycled or composted. Goes in an official **volume-rate bag**.
2. **Food waste (음식물쓰레기)** — almost all leftover food, handled separately.
3. **Recyclables (재활용)** — paper, plastic, glass, metal, cartons, and more, sorted by type.

Mixing these together is the most common mistake foreigners make — and the one most likely to draw a complaint or fine.

## General Waste and the Volume-Rate Bag System

Korea uses a **"pay as you throw" system (종량제, jongnyangje)**. You must buy official **volume-rate trash bags (종량제 봉투)** and put general waste only in those bags.

- **Where to buy:** convenience stores and local supermarkets. Tell them the size you need.
- **Important:** bags are **district-specific (구/동)**. A bag sold in one district may not be valid in another, because the cost funds that district's collection. Buy bags near where you live.
- **Sizes:** sold in a range of liter sizes (small to large) so you pay roughly in proportion to how much you throw away.
- Only waste in an **official bag** is collected. Random bags left out may be ignored or flagged.

## Food Waste: A Category of Its Own

Food waste is collected separately and **must not** go in the general bag. Depending on your building and district, you'll use one of these methods:

- **Designated food-waste bags** (similar concept to volume-rate bags, for food)
- **A communal food-waste bin** with a lid, often using a card or sticker system to charge by weight or volume
- **An RFID smart bin** in some apartment complexes that weighs your deposit and bills accordingly

### What counts as food waste (and what doesn't)

Generally **food waste**: leftover cooked food, vegetable and fruit scraps, rice, noodles, small soft scraps.

Generally **NOT food waste** (these go in general waste): large bones, shells (clams, oysters), egg shells, nut shells, tea bags, fruit pits, onion skins in some districts, and anything hard or not compostable.

> Rules on borderline items vary by district. When unsure, hard/large/inedible items usually belong in the **general waste** bag, not the food bin.

**Tip:** drain liquid from food waste before disposing of it — many systems charge by weight, and water adds weight.

## Recycling: Sort by Material

Recyclables must be **clean, dry, and sorted by type**. Common categories:

| Material | Examples | Prep |
|----------|----------|------|
| **Paper** | Newspaper, boxes, office paper | Flatten boxes; remove tape and labels |
| **Plastic** | PET bottles, containers | Rinse; remove caps/labels where required |
| **Glass** | Bottles, jars | Rinse; remove lids |
| **Cans/Metal** | Drink cans, food tins | Rinse and empty |
| **Cartons** | Milk/juice cartons | Rinse, flatten; often kept separate from paper |
| **Vinyl/Film** | Plastic bags, wrappers | Bundle clean film plastics together |
| **Styrofoam** | Packaging foam | Must be clean; remove tape and labels |

- **Transparent PET bottles** are often collected **separately** from colored plastics — many areas have a dedicated bin for clear PET.
- **Contaminated items** (greasy pizza boxes, dirty containers) usually count as general waste, not recycling.

## Apartment vs. House Disposal

- **Apartment complexes (아파트):** typically have a central collection area with labeled bins and **designated days/times** for recyclables. Follow the posted schedule and bin labels.
- **Villas / houses / officetels:** you usually put bags and sorted recyclables out at the curb on **specific collection nights**. Check with your landlord or neighbors for the local schedule and spot.

If you're new, **ask your landlord, building manager, or a neighbor** to show you the exact spot and days — practice varies block to block.

## Large Items and Special Waste

- **Bulky waste (대형폐기물)** — furniture, mattresses, appliances — requires a **paid sticker/permit** from your district office (often arranged online or by phone). You attach the sticker and leave the item at the designated spot.
- **Batteries, light bulbs, and small electronics** usually have **separate collection bins**, often near apartment recycling areas or community centers.
- **Clothing** can frequently go in **dedicated donation/recycle bins** found on many streets.

## Fines and Why the Rules Matter

Improper disposal — using the wrong bag, mixing food into general waste, or dumping unsorted recyclables — can lead to **fines**. Districts may inspect bags, and apartment managers actively enforce sorting. Beyond fines, careless disposal is a fast way to annoy neighbors. The exact penalty amounts vary by district and year, so treat the rules as strict.

## Quick Survival Checklist

- Buy the **correct district volume-rate bag** for general waste.
- Keep **food waste separate** and drained of liquid.
- **Rinse and sort recyclables** by material; keep clear PET separate.
- Use the **right day and spot** for your building.
- For furniture/appliances, get a **bulky-waste sticker** first.
