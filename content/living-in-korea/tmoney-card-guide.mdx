# T-money Card in Korea

The **T-money card** is Korea's rechargeable transit card, and it's one of the first things you should get when you arrive. Tap it to ride subways and buses across the country, and use it to pay at convenience stores and many small shops. Here's the complete guide for foreigners.

## What Is T-money?

T-money is a **prepaid, rechargeable smart card**. You load it with cash, then tap it on readers to pay. Its main uses:

- **Public transport** — subways, city buses, and many intercity buses
- **Transfers** — Korea's system gives **discounted/free transfers** between buses and subway when you tap in and out
- **Small payments** — convenience stores, some taxis, vending machines, and lockers

It works in **Seoul and across most of Korea**, making it far more convenient than buying single-ride tickets each time.

## Why Foreigners Should Get One

- **No bank account or ARC needed** — it's prepaid with cash, so even day-one tourists can use it.
- **Cheaper and faster** than single-journey tickets.
- **Transfer discounts** save money on multi-leg trips.
- **Works nationwide**, not just in Seoul.

## Where to Buy a T-money Card

- **Convenience stores** — CU, GS25, 7-Eleven, and others sell the cards. Just ask for a "T-money card."
- **Subway station kiosks and machines** — many stations sell and recharge cards.
- **Airports** — available on arrival, handy for your first ride into the city.

The **card itself has a small one-time cost**, separate from the travel credit you load onto it. Designs vary (basic cards, character editions, keychain versions), but they function the same.

> There are also other transit cards (such as Cashbee) that work similarly. T-money is the most widely recognized, but readers generally accept the major Korean transit cards.

## How to Recharge (Top Up)

You **load cash credit** onto the card before traveling:

1. **At a convenience store:** hand the card to the cashier and say how much you want to add. Pay with cash (some stores accept card top-ups).
2. **At a subway recharge machine:** select your language, tap/insert the card, choose an amount, and insert cash.
3. **Top up in set increments** — machines usually offer fixed amounts. Keep a reasonable balance so you're never stuck at a gate.

**Note:** Recharge machines in stations are typically **cash-based**. Carry some cash for topping up, especially outside major hubs.

## How to Use It

### On the subway

1. **Tap the card** on the reader at the fare gate to enter.
2. **Tap again when you exit** — Korean fares depend on distance, so you must tap **both in and out**.
3. Forgetting to tap out can cause a penalty or maximum fare on your next trip.

### On buses

1. **Tap when you board.**
2. **Tap again when you get off**, especially for distance-based or transfer fares — this ensures you get **transfer discounts** and the correct fare.

### Transfers

When you tap out of one leg and tap into the next within the allowed window, the system applies **free or discounted transfers** between buses and subway. This is automatic — just keep tapping in and out.

## Using T-money for Purchases

Beyond transit, you can tap T-money to pay at:

- **Convenience stores**
- **Some vending machines and lockers**
- **Certain taxis** (look for the T-money sticker)
- **Some shops** displaying the T-money logo

It's handy for small change-free purchases, though for larger shopping you'll still use a regular card.

## Checking Your Balance

- **At fare gates**, the reader usually shows your remaining balance when you tap.
- **At convenience stores**, ask the cashier or use the in-store machine.
- **At station machines**, you can check and recharge in one place.

Keep an eye on your balance so you don't get blocked at a gate during rush hour.

## Refunds and Leftover Balance

When you leave Korea (or stop using the card):

- **Small remaining balances** can often be refunded at **convenience stores**, usually up to a limited amount, sometimes minus a small fee.
- **Larger balances** may require going to a designated refund point or service center.
- The **card deposit/purchase cost is generally not refundable** — you keep the physical card.

> Refund limits and procedures vary by location and operator and can change. If you have a large balance, ask at a convenience store or service center about the current refund process before you depart.

## Alternatives and Mobile Options

- **Mobile transit cards:** Some phones support transit cards built into Korean payment apps or Samsung Pay, letting you tap your phone instead of a card. Setup may require a Korean account.
- **Tourist combo cards:** Some prepaid tourist cards (like WOWPASS) also include transit functionality, combining payment and travel in one card.

For most foreigners, a **physical T-money card from a convenience store** remains the simplest, no-strings option.

## Quick Tips

- **Buy it on arrival** at the airport or a convenience store.
- **Keep cash** for topping up at station machines.
- **Always tap in AND out** on subways and buses.
- **Check your balance** before rush hour.
- **Refund leftover credit** at a convenience store before you leave.
