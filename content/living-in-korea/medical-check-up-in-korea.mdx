# Medical Check-ups in Korea for Foreigners

Korea has an efficient, high-quality healthcare system, and **medical check-ups (건강검진)** are a routine part of life here. Foreigners encounter them in two main ways: a **visa/employment health exam**, and **voluntary comprehensive screenings**. Here's what to expect.

## Two Kinds of Check-up

| Type | Purpose | Who Needs It |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **Visa / employment health exam** | Confirm fitness for certain visas/jobs | Some E-2 teachers, certain long-stay categories, some employers |
| **Comprehensive screening (종합검진)** | Voluntary preventive health check | Anyone; often subsidized for insured residents |

## Visa and Employment Health Exams

Some visa categories and employers require a **health check** as part of registration or hiring. This is common for teaching roles and certain long-stay statuses.

### What it typically includes

- **Basic physical** (height, weight, blood pressure, vision)
- **Blood and urine tests**
- **Chest X-ray** (commonly for tuberculosis screening)
- **Drug screening** in some categories
- Sometimes additional tests depending on the visa/job

### How to do it

1. **Ask which hospital is accepted.** Your employer or immigration may specify or recommend an approved hospital — not every clinic's results are accepted for official purposes.
2. **Bring your passport and ARC** (if you have one) and any form your employer/immigration provided.
3. **Fast if required.** Blood tests often need you to **avoid food** for several hours beforehand — confirm when booking.
4. **Complete the tests** (usually finished in one visit, often within a morning).
5. **Collect the certificate/results**, which the hospital issues for submission to your employer or immigration.

> Exact required tests depend on the visa category and the requesting institution. Always confirm the specific checklist and which hospital is accepted before going.

## Comprehensive Health Screenings (종합검진)

Beyond visa exams, Korea is known for thorough **preventive screenings**. Many hospitals offer packaged check-ups ranging from basic to extensive.

### Common tiers

| Tier | Roughly Includes |
|------|------------------|
| **Basic** | Blood/urine tests, blood pressure, basic imaging, vision/hearing |
| **Standard** | Adds ultrasound, more detailed blood panels, ECG |
| **Premium** | Adds endoscopy (gastroscopy/colonoscopy), CT, detailed cancer markers |

Costs vary widely by tier and hospital, from modest amounts for a basic package to substantially more for premium imaging-heavy packages. **Prices and contents change**, so request a current package menu from the hospital.

### National Health Insurance screenings

Residents enrolled in **National Health Insurance (NHIS)** are often eligible for **subsidized periodic health screenings**. If you're insured, check whether you qualify for a covered check-up — it can be free or low-cost. Eligibility and the screening schedule depend on your enrollment and age group.

## What to Expect on the Day

- **Fasting:** Many tests (blood work, endoscopy, abdominal ultrasound) require fasting — typically no food for several hours, sometimes from the night before.
- **Time:** A basic exam can be quick; a comprehensive one with endoscopy may take a few hours.
- **Sedation:** For endoscopy, sedation is commonly offered (you'll need someone to help you home, or to rest, afterward).
- **Results:** Some results are same-day; detailed reports may follow in days, sometimes available online or by mail.

## Finding English-Speaking Care

Large hospitals in major cities often have **international healthcare centers** specifically for foreign patients, with English-speaking coordinators who help with booking, translation, and paperwork. When choosing where to go:

- Look for hospitals advertising an **international clinic / global center**.
- **Call ahead** to confirm English support and to book — this avoids surprises.
- Bring your **passport, ARC, and insurance details**.
- Ask for an **English copy of your results** if you need them for an employer or for records back home.

## Costs and Insurance

- **With NHIS:** Many services are significantly cheaper, and eligible screenings may be covered.
- **Without insurance:** You pay out of pocket; Korea's prices are often reasonable by international standards, but premium packages add up.
- **Private insurance / travel insurance:** Check what's reimbursable and keep itemized receipts.

> Specific prices vary by hospital, package, and year. Always ask for a written quote before committing to a comprehensive screening.

## Practical Tips

- **Confirm the accepted hospital** for any official/visa exam before paying.
- **Ask about fasting** when you book — showing up un-prepared can mean rescheduling.
- **Use an international clinic** if your Korean is limited.
- **Keep copies** of certificates and results; you may need them for visa, employer, or personal records.
- **Don't skip subsidized screenings** if you're insured — they're a real benefit of the system.
