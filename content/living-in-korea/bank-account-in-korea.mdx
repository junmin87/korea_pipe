# Opening a Bank Account in Korea as a Foreigner

A Korean bank account makes resident life dramatically easier — it's how you receive salary, pay rent, set up Korean payment apps, and handle utilities. Here's exactly what you need and which banks are the most foreigner-friendly.

## What You'll Need

For a **full account** with a debit card, online banking, and transfers, most banks ask for:

- **Passport**
- **Alien Registration Card (ARC)** — the single most important document
- **Korean phone number** — needed for verification and the banking app
- **Proof of address or employment** — some banks request a lease, a certificate of employment, or proof of enrollment

> Without an ARC, your options are limited. Some banks will open a **restricted/basic account** for new arrivals, but full features (online transfers, higher limits) usually require an ARC. See the "No ARC Yet?" section below.

Foreigner experience varies a lot by branch and by staff English ability. As a general guide:

**Practical tip:** In big cities, look for branches in areas with many foreigners (near universities, business districts, or expat neighborhoods). Staff there handle foreigner accounts daily and the process is smoother.

## App-Only (Internet) Banks

Korea's app-based banks are popular for their simple sign-up and clean English-friendly interfaces:

| Bank | What It Offers |
|------|----------------|
| **KakaoBank** | Online-only bank; quick app setup; integrates with KakaoTalk |
| **Toss** | Mobile banking, transfers, and payments in one app |
| **K Bank** | Online bank, often used alongside other services |

These still require an **ARC** and a **Korean phone number** to open a full account, but the onboarding is typically faster and more self-service than a traditional branch.

## Step-by-Step: Opening an Account at a Branch

1. **Bring your documents** — passport, ARC, and proof of your Korean phone number. Add proof of address/employment if you have it.
2. **Go to a foreigner-friendly branch**, ideally mid-morning on a weekday to avoid lunchtime crowds.
3. **Take a number** and tell the teller you want to open an account (계좌 개설). If English is limited, a translation app helps.
4. **Fill out the forms** — the teller will guide you. You'll set a PIN for your card and online banking.
5. **Receive your debit/check card.** Some branches print it on the spot; others mail it within a few days.
6. **Set up the mobile app** using the security credentials the bank provides.

The whole visit usually takes **30–60 minutes** once you're called.

## Understanding Transfer Limits for New Accounts

To combat fraud, Korean banks often place **temporary limits on new accounts** — for example, capped daily transfer amounts for the first weeks or months. This is normal. Limits are typically raised once you've used the account for a while or after you provide additional documents (like proof of income or employment). If you need higher limits immediately, ask the branch what documentation lifts the restriction.

## Online Banking and Security

Korean online banking has historically relied on extra security layers. Today the experience is far smoother through banking apps, but expect:

- **App-based authentication** instead of older certificate systems
- **PIN, pattern, or biometric login**
- **One-time verification** via your registered phone number

Set up the app in-branch if you can, so staff can help if a step fails.

## Sending Money Abroad

For international remittance:

- **Traditional banks** can wire money overseas, but rates and fees vary and the paperwork can be heavier.
- **Specialized services** are often cheaper for personal transfers. Availability for foreign residents depends on your status and documentation.
- For larger transfers, banks may ask for **proof of the source of funds** (such as a pay slip), in line with reporting rules.

Compare the total cost (exchange rate margin **plus** fees), not just the headline fee.

## No ARC Yet? Your Options

If you've just arrived and don't have an ARC, you can still handle money:

- **Open a basic/restricted account** at a bank that allows it for newcomers — useful for receiving a first salary, with limited features until you upgrade with an ARC.
- **Use a prepaid card** designed for foreigners, such as **WOWPASS**, which you load with cash and use like a debit card at most merchants and for transit. This is a good bridge until your ARC arrives.
- **Use international cards and ATMs** for day-to-day spending (see our ATM guide below).

## Tips for a Smooth Experience

- **Get your phone number first.** Many bank steps depend on SMS verification.
- **Bring everything, even "maybe" documents.** An extra employment certificate or lease can unlock a full account on the first try.
- **Be patient with limits.** New-account transfer caps are standard, not a sign you did something wrong.
- **Keep your ARC number private.** It's sensitive, like a national ID number.
