# Korean Visa Types Explained

Korea's visa system uses letter-and-number codes (like **E-2**, **D-2**, **F-6**) that signal what you're allowed to do — study, work, join family, or settle. This guide gives you the big-picture map so you can identify which category fits your situation. Always confirm specifics on the **Hi Korea** immigration portal, as rules change.

## How the System Works

Each visa has a **letter** (broad purpose) and a **number** (specific subtype). The letter groups tell you the general intent:

| Letter Group | General Purpose |
|--------------|-----------------|
| **B** | Visa-free / visa-on-arrival short visits |
| **C** | Short-term stays (tourism, short business) |
| **D** | Long-term non-work: study, training, investment, job-seeking |
| **E** | Employment / work |
| **F** | Family, residence, and long-term/permanent settlement |
| **H** | Working holiday and similar |

Your visa controls **how long you can stay** and **what activities are legal** — most importantly, whether and how you can work.

## Short-Term Visas (Tourism and Visits)

- **Visa-free / C categories:** Many nationalities can visit Korea for tourism or short business for a limited period without a long-term visa, often via an electronic travel authorization. These do **not** allow employment and don't lead to an ARC.
- **Use case:** holidays, short business trips, visiting family briefly.

> Visa-free eligibility and electronic authorization requirements depend on your nationality and change periodically. Check current entry rules before you fly.

## Student and Training Visas (D group)

| Visa | Who It's For |
|------|--------------|
| **D-2** | Degree students at universities/colleges |
| **D-4** | Language training and certain non-degree study |
| **D-10** | Job-seekers looking for work or preparing to start a business |

Student visas usually allow **limited part-time work** under conditions (hours and approval requirements). **D-10** is specifically for staying in Korea to interview and transition into employment.

## Work Visas (E group)

These require, in most cases, an **employer to sponsor** you. Common types:

| Visa | Typical Roles |
|------|---------------|
| **E-1** | Professors |
| **E-2** | Foreign language instructors (e.g., English teachers) |
| **E-3** | Researchers |
| **E-6** | Arts, entertainment, sports |
| **E-7** | Skilled/specialized workers in designated occupations |
| **E-9** | Non-professional employment under specific labor programs |

**E-2** (teaching) and **E-7** (specialized/skilled) are the categories most foreigners encounter. With a work visa, you can generally only do the work it specifies for the sponsoring employer; changing jobs often requires updating your status.

## Family and Residence Visas (F group)

The F group offers the **broadest freedoms**, often including the right to work without a separate employer sponsor:

| Visa | Who It's For | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| **F-1** | Visiting/cohabiting family | Limited work rights |
| **F-2** | Long-term residents | Often broad work rights |
| **F-4** | Overseas Koreans (ethnic Korean foreign nationals) | Wide activity range |
| **F-5** | Permanent residents | Most freedoms; no fixed expiry like temporary visas |
| **F-6** | Spouses of Korean nationals | Generally free to work |

F visas are the goal for many long-term residents because they decouple your right to stay from a single employer.

## Working Holiday (H group)

- **H-1 (working holiday):** available to young people from countries with a working-holiday agreement with Korea. It allows travel plus limited work for a set period. Eligibility, age limits, and quotas depend on the bilateral agreement.

## Business and Investment (D group)

- **D-8 (corporate investment)** and **D-9 (trade/business)** support foreigners investing in or operating businesses in Korea.
- Korea also has **startup-oriented tracks** aimed at founders and tech entrepreneurs, sometimes with support programs attached.

## Changing or Extending Your Status

- **Extensions:** Apply through immigration (book via Hi Korea) **before your current period of stay expires**. Don't overstay.
- **Change of status:** You can sometimes switch categories from within Korea — for example, from a job-seeking D-10 to a work E-7 once an employer sponsors you — subject to eligibility.
- **ARC required:** If you hold a long-term visa and stay over 90 days, you'll register for an **Alien Registration Card** that reflects your status.

## How to Choose / Confirm Your Visa

1. **Identify your purpose** — study, work, family, business, or short visit.
2. **Match it to a letter group**, then the specific subtype.
3. **Check the exact requirements** for that subtype on Hi Korea (documents, sponsor, conditions).
4. **Confirm work rights.** Never work outside what your visa allows.
5. **Track expiry dates** and apply for extensions or changes early.

> This guide is an overview, not legal advice. Visa subtypes, eligibility, and conditions change. For your specific case, verify on Hi Korea or consult immigration directly.
