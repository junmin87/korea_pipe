# Finding a Job in Korea as a Foreigner

Working in Korea as a foreigner is very doable, but the path depends on your skills, language ability, and — critically — your **visa**. You can only work legally in roles your visa permits. Here's how to find a job and stay on the right side of the rules.

## First: Your Visa Decides What You Can Do

In Korea, **employment is tied to your visa category**. You cannot simply take any job; the work must match the activity your status allows. Common work-related categories include:

| Common Category | Typical Use |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **E-2** | Foreign language instruction (teaching English and other languages) |
| **E-7** | Skilled/specialized roles sponsored by an employer |
| **D-10** | Job-seeking status, to look for work and attend interviews |
| **F-2 / F-5 / F-6** | Residence and permanent-residence categories with broader work freedom |
| **D-2 / D-4** | Student visas, with limited part-time work allowed under conditions |

> Visa rules and permitted activities change. Always confirm what your specific visa allows on the **Hi Korea** portal before accepting work. Working outside your visa's scope can jeopardize your stay.

If you're job-hunting, the **D-10 job-seeking visa** is designed to let qualified people stay and interview. Many people then switch to a work visa (often E-7) once an employer sponsors them.

## The Most Common Jobs for Foreigners

### English (and other language) teaching

Teaching remains the most common entry point. Options include:

- **Public schools** (often through programs that place foreign teachers)
- **Private academies (학원, "hagwon")**
- **Universities** (usually requiring higher qualifications)
- **Private tutoring** — note this often has visa restrictions; confirm legality for your status

Typical requirements for teaching English: a **bachelor's degree**, citizenship from a recognized native-English country for E-2, and a clean **background check**.

### Professional and specialized roles

With an E-7 or a residence visa, foreigners work in fields such as:

- **IT and software engineering**
- **Design and content/marketing** (especially with bilingual skills)
- **Trade, logistics, and manufacturing specialists**
- **Research and academia**
- **Finance and consulting** at multinational firms

Korean language ability dramatically widens your options outside English-first roles.

### Other paths

- **Translation and interpretation**
- **Content creation, modeling, and media** (status-dependent)
- **Hospitality and tourism** in foreigner-facing roles
- **Startups** — Korea has visa tracks aimed at founders and tech talent

## Where to Look for Jobs

- **Specialized expat job boards** and communities focused on Korea
- **General Korean job platforms** (some have English filters; many roles are posted in Korean)
- **Company career pages**, especially for multinationals and large Korean firms with global teams
- **LinkedIn**, widely used by recruiters hiring bilingual and specialized talent
- **University career centers**, if you studied in Korea
- **Recruiters/agencies**, common for teaching placements and some specialized roles
- **Word of mouth** — networking in your industry and expat communities is genuinely effective

## How the Hiring Process Works

1. **Apply** with a resume tailored to the role. For Korean companies, a clean, concise resume and (sometimes) a cover letter are expected.
2. **Interview** — often one or more rounds, increasingly including video interviews for international candidates.
3. **Offer and contract** — review the terms carefully (salary, hours, housing if provided, severance, notice period).
4. **Visa sponsorship** — for most work visas, the **employer sponsors you**. They'll provide documents you need for the visa application or change of status.
5. **Visa issuance / change of status** — you either get the visa abroad or change your status within Korea via immigration, depending on your situation.
6. **Register / update your ARC** to reflect your employment status once you're working.

## What Employers Usually Ask For

Depending on the role, be ready to provide:

- **Degree certificate** (often notarized/apostilled for teaching roles)
- **Transcripts**
- **Background/criminal record check** (commonly apostilled)
- **Resume and references**
- **Passport** and, if already in Korea, your **ARC**
- **Health check** for certain categories

Getting documents apostilled in your home country **before** arriving saves a lot of trouble.

## Practical Tips

- **Sort your visa strategy first.** Know which category fits you and whether you need an employer to sponsor it.
- **Learn some Korean.** Even basic ability expands your options and helps in daily work life.
- **Mind contract details.** Check working hours, severance (퇴직금), and housing terms — especially in teaching contracts.
- **Beware roles that ask you to work off-visa.** If something sounds informal or "cash only," it may put your status at risk.
- **Keep documents ready.** Apostilled degree and background check are the usual bottlenecks.

## Realistic Expectations

The job market rewards either **English ability** (teaching, global teams) or **specialized skills plus some Korean** (tech, design, trade). Without either, options narrow. The most reliable path is to match a genuine skill to a visa category, then let an employer sponsor you.
