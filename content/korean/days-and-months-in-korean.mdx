# Days of the Week and Months in Korean

Here is the quick answer: the days of the week in Korean all end in **요일 (yoil)**, and the months are simply the **Sino-Korean number plus 월 (wol)**. Once you know the numbers 1 through 12, you already know every month. Let's build out your whole calendar vocabulary.

## Days of the Week (요일)

Each day is named after a natural element — a poetic system shared with Chinese and Japanese.

| English | Hangul | Romanization | Literal element |
|---------|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| Monday | 월요일 | woryoil | Moon |
| Tuesday | 화요일 | hwayoil | Fire |
| Wednesday | 수요일 | suyoil | Water |
| Thursday | 목요일 | mogyoil | Wood |
| Friday | 금요일 | geumyoil | Metal/Gold |
| Saturday | 토요일 | toyoil | Earth |
| Sunday | 일요일 | iryoil | Sun |

To ask "What day is it?" say **무슨 요일이에요?** (museun yoirieyo?). To answer, just give the day: **오늘은 화요일이에요.** (oneureun hwayoirieyo.) — "Today is Tuesday."

The word for "weekend" is **주말 (jumal)**, and "weekday" is **평일 (pyeongil)**.

## Months of the Year (월)

Months could not be simpler: take the Sino-Korean number and add 월 (wol, "month"). Watch the two small spelling quirks for June and October.

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| January | 1월 (일월) | irwol |
| February | 2월 (이월) | iwol |
| March | 3월 (삼월) | samwol |
| April | 4월 (사월) | sawol |
| May | 5월 (오월) | owol |
| June | 6월 (유월) | yuwol |
| July | 7월 (칠월) | chirwol |
| August | 8월 (팔월) | parwol |
| September | 9월 (구월) | guwol |
| October | 10월 (시월) | siwol |
| November | 11월 (십일월) | sibirwol |
| December | 12월 (십이월) | sibiwol |

**The two exceptions:** June is **유월 (yuwol)**, not 육월, and October is **시월 (siwol)**, not 십월. The final consonant is dropped to make them easier to say. Everything else follows the number perfectly.

## Saying Full Dates

Korean dates go from **largest to smallest**: year, then month, then day. The day uses the Sino-Korean number plus 일 (il, "day").

- Year: number + 년 (nyeon)
- Month: number + 월 (wol)
- Day: number + 일 (il)

Example: **2026년 6월 10일** reads as **이천이십육 년 유월 십 일** (icheon-isibyuk nyeon yuwol sib il) — "June 10, 2026."

To ask the date: **오늘 며칠이에요?** (oneul myeochirieyo?) — "What's the date today?"

## Useful Time-Reference Words

These everyday words pair naturally with days and dates:

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| today | 오늘 | oneul |
| tomorrow | 내일 | naeil |
| yesterday | 어제 | eoje |
| the day after tomorrow | 모레 | more |
| this week | 이번 주 | ibeon ju |
| next week | 다음 주 | daeum ju |
| last week | 지난주 | jinanju |
| this month | 이번 달 | ibeon dal |
| next month | 다음 달 | daeum dal |
| every day | 매일 | maeil |

Note that "week" is 주 (ju) and "month" (as a counter of time, not a calendar name) is 달 (dal), a Native Korean word distinct from 월.

## Example Sentences

- **내일은 금요일이에요.** (naeireun geumyoirieyo.) — "Tomorrow is Friday."
- **제 생일은 시월 이십일 일이에요.** (je saengireun siwol isibil irieyo.) — "My birthday is October 20th."
- **이번 주말에 뭐 해요?** (ibeon jumare mwo haeyo?) — "What are you doing this weekend?"
- **회의는 수요일에 있어요.** (hoeuineun suyoire isseoyo.) — "The meeting is on Wednesday."

Notice the particle **에 (e)** marks "on/at" a time: 수요일에 means "on Wednesday."

## A Quick Memory Trick

The days follow the order Moon, Fire, Water, Wood, Metal, Earth, Sun — the same five elements plus the moon and sun. If you remember the elements in order, you can rebuild the whole week. For months, just count: every month is its number plus 월, with only 유월 and 시월 bending the rule.

## Key Takeaways

- Days end in **요일** and are named for the moon, sun, and five elements.
- Months are **Sino-Korean number + 월** — only **유월 (June)** and **시월 (October)** are irregular.
- Dates run largest to smallest: **년 → 월 → 일**.
- The day number uses **Sino-Korean** numbers plus 일.
- Use the particle **에** to say "on" a day or date.
