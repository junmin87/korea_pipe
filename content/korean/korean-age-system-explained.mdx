# The Korean Age System: Why Koreans Count Age Differently

For most of its history, Korea counted age differently from the rest of the world: a baby was **1 year old at birth**, and **everyone gained a year on New Year's Day** — not on their birthday. That made a Korean's stated age typically **one or two years higher** than their international age. Then, in **June 2023**, Korea legally standardized on international age. Here is how it all works.

## The Quick Answer

- **Traditional "Korean age":** born at 1, +1 every January 1st. Usually 1–2 years more than your Western age.
- **International age:** the system used worldwide — 0 at birth, +1 on each birthday.
- **As of June 28, 2023:** Korea adopted international age as the official standard for legal and administrative purposes.

## How Traditional Korean Age Worked

Two rules defined the traditional system, often called **세는 나이 (sneun nai)**, "counting age":

1. **You are 1 at birth.** A newborn is counted as one year old, not zero. (One common explanation links this to counting time in the womb, though the exact origin is debated.)
2. **Everyone ages on January 1st.** Instead of aging on your birthday, the whole country adds a year together at New Year.

Because of this, a baby born on December 31 would be **1** at birth, then turn **2** the very next day on January 1 — even though only one day had passed in real time.

## How to Calculate Your Korean Age

The traditional formula is simple:

- **Korean age = (current year − birth year) + 1**

| Your situation | International age | Traditional Korean age |
|----------------|------------------|------------------------|
| Born 2000, birthday already passed this year | 26 (in 2026) | 27 |
| Born 2000, birthday not yet this year | 25 (in 2026) | 27 |
| Born Dec 2025, on Jan 1 2026 | 0 | 2 |

Notice the traditional age ignores your birthday entirely — it only cares about the calendar year.

## A Third System: "Year Age" (연 나이)

To complicate matters, Korea also used a middle system called **연 나이 (yeon nai)**, "year age," for certain laws like military service and school entry:

- **Year age = current year − birth year**

This counts you as 0 in your birth year and adds 1 each January 1, landing between the international and traditional counts. So before 2023, a single person could technically have **three different ages** depending on context.

| System | Korean name | Formula | Example (born 2000, in 2026) |
|--------|-------------|---------|------------------------------|
| Traditional | 세는 나이 | year − birth + 1 | 27 |
| Year age | 연 나이 | year − birth | 26 |
| International | 만 나이 | by birthday | 25 or 26 |

The international age is called **만 나이 (man nai)** in Korean — the word **만 (man)** signals "full/completed" years.

## The 2023 Legal Change

On **June 28, 2023**, a revised law took effect making **만 나이 (international age) the unified legal and administrative standard** across Korea. The goal was to end confusion in contracts, medical records, and official documents where the different systems clashed.

What this means in practice:

- **Legal and official documents now use international age.** When forms ask your age, give your international (만) age.
- **Many everyday conversations are shifting too,** though old habits linger and some people still mention their traditional age casually.
- **A few specific laws still use "year age" (연 나이),** such as eligibility tied to birth year for things like youth-related rules and conscription timing. These were not all converted to 만 나이.

So the headline is: officially, Korea now matches the world, but you may still hear traditional age in casual talk.

## Why Age Matters So Much in Korea

Age is not just small talk in Korea — it shapes **how people speak to each other**. Korean has speech levels and honorifics tied to seniority, so knowing who is older determines:

- Which **speech level** to use (formal, polite, or casual).
- Which **terms of address** apply (for example, 오빠, 언니, 형, 누나 for older people).
- Social expectations around respect and etiquette.

This is why Koreans often ask your age early in a relationship — they are figuring out the correct way to address you, not being nosy.

## Useful Age Vocabulary

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| age | 나이 | nai |
| international age | 만 나이 | man nai |
| How old are you? (polite) | 나이가 어떻게 되세요? | naiga eotteoke doeseyo? |
| years old (counter) | 살 | sal |
| same age | 동갑 | donggap |

Age uses **Native Korean numbers** plus the counter **살 (sal)**:

- **저는 스물다섯 살이에요.** (jeoneun seumul-daseot sarieyo.) — "I am twenty-five years old."
- **우리는 동갑이에요.** (urineun donggabieyo.) — "We're the same age."

## Example Conversation

- A: **나이가 어떻게 되세요?** (naiga eotteoke doeseyo?) — "How old are you?"
- B: **만으로 스물여섯 살이에요.** (maneuro seumul-yeoseot sarieyo.) — "I'm twenty-six in international age."

Adding **만으로 (maneuro, "in full/international count")** makes it clear which system you mean — handy during this transition period.

## Key Takeaways

- Traditional **Korean age** counts a newborn as 1 and adds a year every January 1.
- The formula is **(current year − birth year) + 1**, making it 1–2 years above Western age.
- Korea historically used **three systems**: 세는 나이, 연 나이, and 만 나이.
- Since **June 28, 2023**, **international age (만 나이)** is the official legal standard.
- Age determines speech level and forms of address, which is why it comes up so early.
