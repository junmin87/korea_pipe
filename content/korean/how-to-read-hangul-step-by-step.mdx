# How to Read Hangul: A Step-by-Step Guide for Beginners

You can learn to **read Hangul in a single sitting**. Korean is written with an alphabet, not thousands of characters, and that alphabet was deliberately designed to be easy. The secret is understanding that Korean letters are grouped into **syllable blocks**. Follow these steps in order and you will be sounding out words by the end of this page.

## Step 1: Understand the Big Idea — Syllable Blocks

Unlike English, where letters sit in a straight line, Korean letters stack into square **syllable blocks**. Each block is one syllable and contains a starting consonant, a vowel, and sometimes a final consonant.

- 한 is one block: ㅎ + ㅏ + ㄴ.
- 국 is one block: ㄱ + ㅜ + ㄱ.
- Together, 한국 (hanguk) means "Korea."

You read a block in a fixed order: **top-left first, then the vowel, then the bottom**. Master that reading path and the rest is learning the letter sounds.

## Step 2: Learn the Basic Vowels

Vowels are the heart of each block. Notice the pattern: a vertical line means the vowel sits to the **right** of the consonant; a horizontal line means it sits **below**.

| Hangul | Romanization | Sounds like |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| ㅏ | a | "ah" in father |
| ㅓ | eo | "uh" in sun |
| ㅗ | o | "oh" in go |
| ㅜ | u | "oo" in moon |
| ㅡ | eu | "oo" with unrounded lips |
| ㅣ | i | "ee" in see |

Adding a short extra stroke makes a "y" version: ㅏ becomes ㅑ (ya), ㅓ becomes ㅕ (yeo), ㅗ becomes ㅛ (yo), ㅜ becomes ㅠ (yu).

## Step 3: Learn the Basic Consonants

Many Korean consonants were shaped to **mimic the mouth** making the sound, which makes them easier to remember.

| Hangul | Romanization | Sounds like |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| ㄱ | g/k | between g and k |
| ㄴ | n | n in no |
| ㄷ | d/t | between d and t |
| ㄹ | r/l | a light r/l |
| ㅁ | m | m in me |
| ㅂ | b/p | between b and p |
| ㅅ | s | s in see |
| ㅇ | silent / ng | silent at the start; "ng" at the end |
| ㅈ | j | j in jump |
| ㅎ | h | h in hello |

The most important trick: **ㅇ is silent at the start of a block**. It is a placeholder so a vowel can stand alone. So 아 is just "a," and 우 is just "u."

## Step 4: Read Consonant + Vowel Blocks

Now combine. Read the consonant, then the vowel.

| Block | Breakdown | Reads as |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 가 | ㄱ + ㅏ | ga |
| 너 | ㄴ + ㅓ | neo |
| 도 | ㄷ + ㅗ | do |
| 무 | ㅁ + ㅜ | mu |
| 이 | ㅇ(silent) + ㅣ | i |

Practice saying each one aloud. Where the vowel has a vertical line (ㅏ, ㅓ, ㅣ), it goes to the right; where it has a horizontal line (ㅗ, ㅜ, ㅡ), it goes underneath.

## Step 5: Add a Final Consonant (Batchim)

A consonant at the **bottom** of a block is called a **batchim (받침)**. You read it last.

| Block | Breakdown | Reads as |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 산 | ㅅ + ㅏ + ㄴ | san |
| 밥 | ㅂ + ㅏ + ㅂ | bap |
| 강 | ㄱ + ㅏ + ㅇ | gang ("ng" sound) |
| 물 | ㅁ + ㅜ + ㄹ | mul |

Here ㅇ in the batchim position is **not** silent — it makes the "ng" sound, as in 강 (gang).

## Step 6: Read Real Words

Put it all together and read these common words. Cover the romanization and try first.

| Word | Breakdown | Romanization | Meaning |
|------|-----------|--------------|---------|
| 한국 | 한 + 국 | hanguk | Korea |
| 사람 | 사 + 람 | saram | person |
| 안녕 | 안 + 녕 | annyeong | hi / peace |
| 김치 | 김 + 치 | gimchi | kimchi |
| 사랑 | 사 + 랑 | sarang | love |

If you can sound those out, you are officially reading Hangul.

## Step 7: Practice Daily for a Week

Reading fluency comes from repetition, not memorizing rules. A simple plan:

1. **Day 1–2:** Drill the vowels and consonants until you recognize each instantly.
2. **Day 3–4:** Read consonant + vowel blocks aloud.
3. **Day 5–7:** Read words with batchim, like signs, menus, and labels.

Reading Korean signs and shop names in real life — or on photos online — is the fastest way to cement it.

## Common Beginner Questions

- **Why does ㅇ have two jobs?** It is silent as the first letter (just a vowel holder) and "ng" as a batchim. Context tells you which.
- **Do I need to learn romanization?** Only as a temporary crutch. The goal is to read Hangul directly, since romanization is inconsistent.
- **What about double consonants and tricky pronunciation?** Those come next, once basic reading is automatic.

## Key Takeaways

- Hangul groups letters into **syllable blocks**, read top-left → vowel → bottom.
- Vertical-line vowels go to the right; horizontal-line vowels go below.
- **ㅇ is silent at the start** and "ng" at the bottom of a block.
- A bottom consonant is a **batchim**, read last.
- Daily reading practice — not rule memorization — builds fluency fast.
