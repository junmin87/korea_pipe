# Basic Korean Sentence Structure

One of the biggest differences between Korean and English is **word order**. English uses Subject-Verb-Object (SVO), but Korean uses **Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)**.

## SVO vs. SOV

| English (SVO) | Korean (SOV) | Literal Translation |
|---|---|---|
| I eat rice. | 저는 밥을 먹어요. | I rice eat. |
| She reads a book. | 그녀는 책을 읽어요. | She book reads. |
| We study Korean. | 우리는 한국어를 공부해요. | We Korean study. |

The verb **always goes at the end** of a Korean sentence. This is the most important rule to remember.

## Particles: The Glue of Korean Sentences

Korean uses **particles** (조사) — small markers attached to words — to show their role in the sentence. This means word order is actually flexible, as long as the verb stays at the end.

### Subject Particles: 은/는 and 이/가

| Particle | When to use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| 은 (eun) | After a consonant | 저**는** (I + topic) |
| 는 (neun) | After a vowel | 나**는** (I + topic, casual) |
| 이 (i) | After a consonant | 책**이** (book + subject) |
| 가 (ga) | After a vowel | 내**가** (I + subject, casual) |

> **은/는** marks the **topic** (what you're talking about). **이/가** marks the **subject** (who does the action). For beginners, you can think of both as "subject markers" — the distinction becomes clearer with practice.

### Object Particles: 을/를

| Particle | When to use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| 을 (eul) | After a consonant | 밥**을** (rice + object) |
| 를 (reul) | After a vowel | 커피**를** (coffee + object) |

## Building Your First Sentences

Follow this formula: **Subject + 은/는 + Object + 을/를 + Verb**

### Examples

**저는 커피를 마셔요.**
- 저 (I) + 는 (topic) + 커피 (coffee) + 를 (object) + 마셔요 (drink)
- "I drink coffee."

**학생은 한국어를 배워요.**
- 학생 (student) + 은 (topic) + 한국어 (Korean) + 를 (object) + 배워요 (learn)
- "The student learns Korean."

**고양이가 물을 마셔요.**
- 고양이 (cat) + 가 (subject) + 물 (water) + 을 (object) + 마셔요 (drinks)
- "The cat drinks water."

## Dropping the Subject

In Korean, you can often **drop the subject** when it's obvious from context. This is very common in everyday speech:

- 밥 먹었어요? — "(Did you) eat?" (subject "you" is implied)
- 좋아해요. — "(I) like (it)." (both subject and object implied)

## Negation

There are two simple ways to make a sentence negative:

1. **안 + Verb:** 안 먹어요 (don't eat)
2. **Verb stem + 지 않아요:** 먹지 않아요 (don't eat — more formal)

## Practice

Try building these sentences in Korean:

1. I study Korean. → 저는 한국어를 공부해요.
2. My friend eats kimchi. → 친구는 김치를 먹어요.
3. We don't drink coffee. → 우리는 커피를 안 마셔요.

## Key Takeaways

- Korean word order is **SOV**: the verb always goes last.
- **Particles** mark the role of each word — learn 은/는, 이/가, and 을/를 first.
- Subjects can be dropped when context makes them clear.
- Start with the informal polite ending (-아요/-어요) for everyday sentences.
