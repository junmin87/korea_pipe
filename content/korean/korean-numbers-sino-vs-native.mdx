# Korean Numbers Explained: Sino-Korean vs Native Korean

Korean uses **two separate number systems**, and you need both. The short answer: use **Sino-Korean** for dates, money, phone numbers, minutes, and anything counted in large amounts; use **Native Korean** for counting things, people, age, and hours on the clock. Once you learn which situation calls for which, the rest is memorization.

## The Two Systems at a Glance

| Number | Sino-Korean | Romanization | Native Korean | Romanization |
|--------|-------------|--------------|---------------|--------------|
| 1 | 일 | il | 하나 | hana |
| 2 | 이 | i | 둘 | dul |
| 3 | 삼 | sam | 셋 | set |
| 4 | 사 | sa | 넷 | net |
| 5 | 오 | o | 다섯 | daseot |
| 6 | 육 | yuk | 여섯 | yeoseot |
| 7 | 칠 | chil | 일곱 | ilgop |
| 8 | 팔 | pal | 여덟 | yeodeol |
| 9 | 구 | gu | 아홉 | ahop |
| 10 | 십 | sip | 열 | yeol |

Native Korean numbers traditionally stop at 99. For 100 and above, Korean uses Sino-Korean numbers only.

## Counting Past Ten (Sino-Korean)

Sino-Korean is wonderfully logical. You build numbers by combining the digits you already know.

| Number | Hangul | Romanization | How it's built |
|--------|--------|--------------|----------------|
| 11 | 십일 | sip-il | 10 + 1 |
| 20 | 이십 | i-sip | 2 + 10 |
| 35 | 삼십오 | sam-sip-o | 3 + 10 + 5 |
| 100 | 백 | baek | hundred |
| 1,000 | 천 | cheon | thousand |
| 10,000 | 만 | man | ten-thousand |

Note that Korean groups large numbers by **ten-thousands (만)**, not thousands. So 100,000 is 십만 (sip-man, "ten ten-thousands"), and 1,000,000 is 백만 (baek-man, "hundred ten-thousands"). This trips up many English speakers, so practice it early.

## Counting Past Ten (Native Korean)

| Number | Hangul | Romanization |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| 11 | 열하나 | yeol-hana |
| 20 | 스물 | seumul |
| 30 | 서른 | seoreun |
| 40 | 마흔 | maheun |
| 50 | 쉰 | swin |
| 60 | 예순 | yesun |
| 70 | 일흔 | ilheun |
| 80 | 여든 | yeodeun |
| 90 | 아흔 | aheun |

Unlike Sino-Korean, the tens have unique names you simply have to memorize.

## When to Use Which System

This is the part that matters most. Here is a quick reference:

| Use Sino-Korean for | Use Native Korean for |
|---------------------|------------------------|
| Dates (year, month, day) | Counting objects (with counters) |
| Money (won) | Counting people |
| Phone numbers | Age (traditional style) |
| Minutes and seconds | The hour on a clock |
| Addresses | Number of times |
| Math and measurements | |

## Telling Time: Both at Once

Clock time famously uses **both** systems in one sentence. The hour is Native Korean; the minutes are Sino-Korean.

- **3:15** = 세 시 십오 분 (se si sibo bun) — "three (hour) fifteen (minutes)"
- **7:30** = 일곱 시 삼십 분 (ilgop si samsip bun)

Notice that 셋 becomes 세 and 넷 becomes 네 before a counter like 시 (si, "o'clock").

## Counters: The Number Changes Shape

When you count objects in Korean, you use a **counter word** after the number. The most common is 개 (gae) for general items.

| English | Korean | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| one apple | 사과 한 개 | sagwa han gae |
| two people | 두 명 | du myeong |
| three books | 책 세 권 | chaek se gwon |
| four cups (of coffee) | 커피 네 잔 | keopi ne jan |

The first four Native numbers shorten before counters:

| Full form | Before a counter |
|-----------|------------------|
| 하나 (hana) | 한 (han) |
| 둘 (dul) | 두 (du) |
| 셋 (set) | 세 (se) |
| 넷 (net) | 네 (ne) |
| 스물 (seumul) | 스무 (seumu) |

## Example Sentences

- **저는 스물다섯 살이에요.** (jeoneun seumul-daseot sarieyo.) — "I am twenty-five years old." (Native + counter 살)
- **이건 삼천 원이에요.** (igeon samcheon wonieyo.) — "This is 3,000 won." (Sino, money)
- **사과 두 개 주세요.** (sagwa du gae juseyo.) — "Two apples, please." (Native + counter 개)
- **지금 다섯 시예요.** (jigeum daseot siyeyo.) — "It's five o'clock now." (Native hour)

## Quick Practice

Try saying these out loud:

1. Your phone number — Sino-Korean, digit by digit.
2. The current time — Native hour, Sino minutes.
3. How many siblings you have — Native + counter 명 (myeong).

## Key Takeaways

- Korean has two number systems; both are essential.
- **Sino-Korean**: dates, money, minutes, phone numbers, big quantities.
- **Native Korean**: counting things and people, age, and the hour.
- Large numbers group by **만 (ten-thousand)**, not by thousand.
- 하나/둘/셋/넷/스물 shorten to 한/두/세/네/스무 before counters.
