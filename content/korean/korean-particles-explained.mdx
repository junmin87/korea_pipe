# Korean Particles for Beginners: 은/는, 이/가, 을/를

Korean particles are tiny words attached to the end of a noun that tell you the noun's job in the sentence — who is doing the action, what is being acted on, and what the sentence is about. The three you must learn first are the **topic marker (은/는)**, the **subject marker (이/가)**, and the **object marker (을/를)**.

## How Particles Work

In English, word order tells you the role of each word: "Dog bites man" means something different from "Man bites dog." Korean is more flexible because **particles carry that information**, not position. Attach the right particle and the listener knows the role no matter where the word sits.

Each particle has two forms. You choose based on whether the noun ends in a vowel or a consonant (a final consonant is called a **batchim**).

| Role | After a consonant | After a vowel |
|------|-------------------|---------------|
| Topic | 은 (eun) | 는 (neun) |
| Subject | 이 (i) | 가 (ga) |
| Object | 을 (eul) | 를 (reul) |

For example, 책 (chaek, "book") ends in a consonant, so it takes 은/이/을. 사과 (sagwa, "apple") ends in a vowel, so it takes 는/가/를.

## The Topic Marker: 은/는

The topic marker introduces **what the sentence is about**. Think of it as "as for ___" or "speaking of ___." It often sets up a contrast or a general statement.

- **저는 학생이에요.** (jeoneun haksaengieyo.) — "As for me, I'm a student."
- **날씨는 좋아요.** (nalssineun joayo.) — "The weather is nice." (introducing the weather as a topic)

Use 은/는 when you bring up a new topic, make a general statement, or compare two things:

- **커피는 좋아하지만 차는 안 좋아해요.** (keopineun joahajiman chaneun an joahaeyo.) — "I like coffee, but I don't like tea." (contrast)

## The Subject Marker: 이/가

The subject marker points to **who or what performs the action or has the quality**. It answers the question "who?" or "what?" and often highlights new or specific information.

- **고양이가 자요.** (goyangiga jayo.) — "The cat is sleeping."
- **누가 왔어요?** (nuga wasseoyo?) — "Who came?" — **친구가 왔어요.** (chinguga wasseoyo.) — "A friend came."

When someone asks "who" or "what," the answer almost always takes 이/가, because you are identifying the specific subject.

## 은/는 vs 이/가: The Classic Confusion

This is the single hardest point for beginners. Both can mark the subject of a sentence, but the nuance differs.

| 은/는 (topic) | 이/가 (subject) |
|----------------|------------------|
| What the sentence is about | Who/what specifically does it |
| General or known information | New or emphasized information |
| Sets up contrast | Answers "who?" / "what?" |

Compare:

- **제 이름은 민수예요.** (je ireumeun minsuyeyo.) — "My name is Minsu." (introducing the topic "my name")
- **민수가 했어요.** (minsuga haesseoyo.) — "Minsu did it." (emphasizing *who* did it)

A useful rule of thumb: when in doubt for a general "I am / this is" statement, 은/는 usually sounds natural. When you are pinpointing or answering a question, 이/가 fits better.

## The Object Marker: 을/를

The object marker tags the **thing receiving the action** — the direct object. It pairs with action verbs.

- **밥을 먹어요.** (babeul meogeoyo.) — "I eat rice."
- **영화를 봐요.** (yeonghwareul bwayo.) — "I watch a movie."

In casual speech, Koreans often **drop** the object marker, especially 를: 밥 먹어요 still means "I eat." But as a learner, keep using it until the patterns feel natural — it makes your sentences clear.

## Putting It Together

A full Korean sentence often stacks these particles in Subject-Object-Verb order:

- **저는 한국어를 공부해요.** (jeoneun hangugeoreul gongbuhaeyo.) — "I study Korean."
  - 저 + 는 = topic ("as for me")
  - 한국어 + 를 = object ("Korean")
  - 공부해요 = verb ("study")

- **동생이 사과를 먹어요.** (dongsaengi sagwareul meogeoyo.) — "My younger sibling eats an apple."
  - 동생 + 이 = subject
  - 사과 + 를 = object
  - 먹어요 = verb

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Forgetting batchim agreement.** 책는 is wrong; it must be 책은 (consonant ending).
- **Overusing 이/가 for general statements.** "I am a teacher" is 저는 선생님이에요, not 저가.
- **Translating particles literally.** There is no perfect English equivalent for 은/는 — think "as for," not a fixed word.

## Key Takeaways

- Particles mark a noun's role, freeing Korean from strict word order.
- **은/는** = topic ("as for"), known or contrasted information.
- **이/가** = subject, new or emphasized, answers "who/what."
- **을/를** = object, the thing receiving the action.
- Choose the form (은 vs 는, etc.) based on whether the noun ends in a consonant or vowel.
