# Korean Speech Levels: Formal, Polite, and Casual Explained

Korean encodes **respect and social distance** directly into its verb endings. The same sentence changes shape depending on who you are speaking to. As a beginner, you mainly need three levels: **formal (합니다체)**, **polite (해요체)**, and **casual (반말)**. Learn the polite level first — it is safe in almost every situation.

## Why Speech Levels Exist

In Korean, how you speak signals your relationship to the listener: age difference, social rank, and how close you are. Using the wrong level can sound rude or oddly stiff. Rather than memorizing rigid rules, think of it as a dial: turn it up for strangers, elders, and professional settings; turn it down for close friends and younger people you know well.

## The Three Levels Side by Side

Here is the verb "to do" (하다) and "to eat" (먹다) across the levels:

| Level | "I do" | "I eat" | Feel |
|-------|--------|---------|------|
| Formal (합니다체) | 합니다 (hamnida) | 먹습니다 (meokseumnida) | Crisp, official, respectful |
| Polite (해요체) | 해요 (haeyo) | 먹어요 (meogeoyo) | Friendly and respectful |
| Casual (반말) | 해 (hae) | 먹어 (meogeo) | Close, intimate, informal |

## Formal Level: 합니다체

The formal level is used in **public and professional settings**: news broadcasts, business presentations, the military, customer service, and first meetings where you want to sound serious and respectful. It is built by adding **-(스)ㅂ니다** to the verb stem.

- Statements: stem + ㅂ니다 / 습니다
- Questions: stem + ㅂ니까 / 습니까

Examples:

- **감사합니다.** (gamsahamnida.) — "Thank you."
- **어디에 가십니까?** (eodie gasimnikka?) — "Where are you going?"
- **저는 회사원입니다.** (jeoneun hoesawonimnida.) — "I am an office worker."

This level sounds polished but can feel distant in casual conversation.

## Polite Level: 해요체

The polite level is the **everyday workhorse** of spoken Korean. It is respectful without being stiff, and you can use it with almost anyone — shopkeepers, coworkers, new acquaintances, and elders in informal contexts. It is built by adding **-아요/-어요** to the verb stem.

- If the last stem vowel is ㅏ or ㅗ, add -아요.
- Otherwise, add -어요.
- 하다 verbs become 해요.

Examples:

- **어디에 가요?** (eodie gayo?) — "Where are you going?"
- **밥 먹어요.** (bab meogeoyo.) — "I'm eating."
- **한국어 공부해요.** (hangugeo gongbuhaeyo.) — "I study Korean."

If you only learn one level, learn this one. It will carry you through nearly every real conversation.

## Casual Level: 반말

Casual speech, called **반말 (banmal)**, drops the polite ending entirely. You use it with **close friends, siblings, younger people, and children** — people with whom you have an established close or junior relationship. Using 반말 with the wrong person can be offensive, so wait until someone invites you to "speak comfortably" (말 놓다, mal nota).

- Take the polite form and drop 요: 해요 becomes 해, 먹어요 becomes 먹어.

Examples:

- **어디 가?** (eodi ga?) — "Where ya going?"
- **밥 먹었어?** (bab meogeosseo?) — "Did you eat?"
- **나도 좋아.** (nado joa.) — "I like it too."

## When to Use Each Level

| Situation | Recommended level |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Job interview, presentation | Formal (합니다체) |
| Talking to a stranger or shopkeeper | Polite (해요체) |
| Coworkers, new acquaintances | Polite (해요체) |
| Close friends your age | Casual (반말) |
| Younger family or children | Casual (반말) |
| An elder you just met | Formal or Polite |

## Honorifics: A Related Layer

Separate from speech levels, Korean also has **honorific forms** that show respect for the *person being talked about*, often with the infix **-(으)시-** and special vocabulary. For example, 먹다 (to eat) becomes 드시다 (deusida) when the subject is someone you respect:

- **할머니가 진지를 드세요.** (halmeoniga jinjireul deuseyo.) — "Grandmother is eating (her meal)." (honorific verb 드시다 and honorific noun 진지)

You can combine honorifics with any speech level. Beginners can recognize a few common ones (드시다, 주무시다 for sleep, 계시다 for "to be/stay") and add more over time.

## A Safe Strategy for Beginners

1. **Default to the polite 해요 level.** It is respectful and rarely wrong.
2. **Switch up to formal 합니다체** for interviews, speeches, and very formal settings.
3. **Only drop to 반말** when the relationship clearly allows it, or when the other person uses it with you first.

## Key Takeaways

- Korean verb endings change with social distance and respect.
- **Formal (합니다체)**: public, professional, official.
- **Polite (해요체)**: the everyday default — learn this first.
- **Casual (반말)**: close friends and juniors only.
- Honorifics are a separate layer that can combine with any level.
