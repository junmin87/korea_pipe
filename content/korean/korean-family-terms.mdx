# Korean Family Terms: Mom, Dad, Brother, Sister, and More

Korean family vocabulary has one rule that surprises every beginner: **the word for an older sibling depends on the speaker's gender.** A woman and a man use different words for "older brother." Beyond that, family terms are warm, frequently used, and even extended to close friends. Here is everything you need, organized by relationship.

## Parents

| English | Hangul | Romanization | Note |
|---------|--------|--------------|------|
| mom (casual) | 엄마 | eomma | what you call her directly |
| mother (formal) | 어머니 | eomeoni | polite/formal |
| dad (casual) | 아빠 | appa | what you call him directly |
| father (formal) | 아버지 | abeoji | polite/formal |
| parents | 부모님 | bumonim | -님 adds respect |

In daily life, children call their parents **엄마** and **아빠**. The forms **어머니** and **아버지** are more formal and respectful, used in writing or when speaking carefully.

## Siblings — The Gender Rule

This is the most important table on the page. The word you use for an **older** sibling changes depending on whether **you** (the speaker) are male or female.

| Older sibling | If you are male | If you are female |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| older brother | 형 (hyeong) | 오빠 (oppa) |
| older sister | 누나 (nuna) | 언니 (eonni) |

For **younger** siblings, gender of the speaker does not matter:

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| younger sibling (general) | 동생 | dongsaeng |
| younger brother | 남동생 | namdongsaeng |
| younger sister | 여동생 | yeodongsaeng |

A memory aid: **a girl says 오빠 and 언니; a boy says 형 and 누나.** These four words are everywhere in Korean dramas and pop culture.

### Sibling Words for Friends

Koreans use 오빠, 언니, 형, and 누나 for close older friends too, not just blood relatives. A younger woman may call an older male friend **오빠**, and a younger man may call an older male friend **형**. It signals warmth and respect for age.

## Grandparents

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| grandmother | 할머니 | halmeoni |
| grandfather | 할아버지 | harabeoji |

To specify the maternal side, add **외 (oe)**: 외할머니 (maternal grandmother), 외할아버지 (maternal grandfather). The paternal side is the default, or can be marked with 친 (chin).

## Aunts, Uncles, and Extended Family

Korean is very specific about extended family, distinguishing the mother's side from the father's side. Here are the most common terms:

| English | Hangul | Romanization | Side |
|---------|--------|--------------|------|
| paternal uncle | 삼촌 | samchon | father's brother |
| paternal aunt | 고모 | gomo | father's sister |
| maternal uncle | 외삼촌 | oesamchon | mother's brother |
| maternal aunt | 이모 | imo | mother's sister |
| aunt (uncle's wife) | 숙모 | sukmo | by marriage |

The words **이모 (imo)** and **삼촌 (samchon)** are also used affectionately for close family friends and even restaurant staff in casual settings.

## Children and Spouse

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| son | 아들 | adeul |
| daughter | 딸 | ttal |
| child / kid | 아이 / 애 | ai / ae |
| husband | 남편 | nampyeon |
| wife | 아내 | anae |
| spouse | 배우자 | baeuja |

## In-Laws (a Brief Look)

In-law terms are detailed in Korean, and even native speakers sometimes look them up. A few common ones:

| English | Hangul | Romanization |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| father-in-law (wife's father) | 장인 | jangin |
| mother-in-law (wife's mother) | 장모 | jangmo |
| father-in-law (husband's father) | 시아버지 | siabeoji |
| mother-in-law (husband's mother) | 시어머니 | sieomeoni |

The prefix **시 (si)** generally relates to the husband's family; terms with **처/장** relate to the wife's family.

## The Word for "Family"

The general word for family is **가족 (gajok)**. To talk about your whole family politely, you can say **우리 가족 (uri gajok)** — "my/our family." Koreans often use **우리 (uri, "our")** where English would say "my," as in 우리 엄마 ("my mom").

## Example Sentences

- **우리 가족은 네 명이에요.** (uri gajogeun ne myeongieyo.) — "My family has four people."
- **저는 오빠가 한 명 있어요.** (jeoneun oppaga han myeong isseoyo.) — "I (a female speaker) have one older brother."
- **할머니가 김치를 만들어요.** (halmeoniga gimchireul mandeureoyo.) — "Grandmother makes kimchi."
- **이모, 여기 물 좀 주세요.** (imo, yeogi mul jom juseyo.) — "Auntie (to staff/friend), some water here please."

## Key Takeaways

- **엄마/아빠** are casual; **어머니/아버지** are formal.
- For older siblings, a **female says 오빠/언니** and a **male says 형/누나**.
- Younger siblings use **동생** (남동생 / 여동생) regardless of speaker gender.
- Korean distinguishes **maternal vs paternal** relatives (이모 vs 고모, 외삼촌 vs 삼촌).
- Sibling and aunt/uncle terms are extended to **close friends** as a sign of warmth.
