# How to Say Hello in Korean

The standard way to say hello in Korean is **안녕하세요** (annyeonghaseyo). But just like "thank you," Korean has different greeting forms depending on the situation, your relationship, and even the communication medium.

## The Core Greetings at a Glance

| Korean | Romanization | Formality | Situation |
|--------|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| 안녕하십니까 | annyeonghasimnikka | Very formal | Speeches, news, military, formal events |
| 안녕하세요 | annyeonghaseyo | Polite | Default for almost everyone |
| 안녕 | annyeong | Casual | Close friends, same age or younger |
| 여보세요 | yeoboseyo | Polite | Answering the phone only |

## 안녕하세요 (Annyeonghaseyo) — The Universal Hello

This is the greeting you'll use 90% of the time. It works with strangers, coworkers, shop owners, your friend's parents — virtually anyone.

**Literal meaning:** "Are you at peace?" (안녕 = peace/well-being, 하세요 = polite "do you")

### When to Use It

- Meeting someone for the first time
- Entering a shop or restaurant
- Greeting neighbors
- Starting a conversation with anyone you're not extremely close to
- Any situation where you're unsure of the formality level

### How to Bow

In Korean culture, greetings often come with a bow:

- **Casual bow (목례):** A slight nod — with acquaintances, passing someone in a hallway
- **Standard bow (인사):** Bend at the waist about 15–30 degrees — general greeting for most situations
- **Deep bow (큰절):** 45–90 degrees — formal occasions, meeting elders during holidays like 설날 (Lunar New Year)

You don't need to bow deeply every time you say 안녕하세요. A slight nod or standard bow is fine for daily life.

## 안녕하십니까 (Annyeonghasimnikka) — Very Formal Hello

This is the most formal version. You'll hear it from:

- News anchors and TV presenters
- Military service members
- At formal ceremonies and official events
- In very formal business presentations

Unless you work in these environments, you won't need to use this form actively — but it's good to recognize it.

## 안녕 (Annyeong) — Casual Hello (and Bye)

The casual form drops the polite ending entirely. Use it only with:

- Close friends of the same age or younger
- Children
- People who've agreed to speak casually with you (반말, banmal)

**Important:** 안녕 doubles as both "hello" and "goodbye" in casual Korean.

| Situation | What You Say |
|-----------|-------------|
| Seeing a close friend | 안녕! |
| Leaving a close friend | 안녕! / 잘 가! (jal ga — "go well") |

> **Warning:** Don't use 안녕 with someone older, a coworker you're not close to, or anyone in a service role. Use 안녕하세요 instead.

## 여보세요 (Yeoboseyo) — Phone Hello

This greeting is **exclusively for answering phone calls**. Don't use it face-to-face — it will sound very strange.

| Situation | Appropriate Greeting |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Answering a phone call | 여보세요 |
| Calling someone and they pick up | 여보세요, [name]씨? |
| Meeting someone in person | 안녕하세요 (NOT 여보세요) |

**여보세요** comes from 여기 보세요 ("look here"), an old way of getting someone's attention. Over time it became the standard phone greeting.

You might also hear people say **여보세요?** with a rising tone when they think the line went quiet — it's the Korean equivalent of "Hello? Are you there?"

## Time-Based Greetings

Korean doesn't rely on time-based greetings the way English does ("good morning," "good evening"). **안녕하세요** works at any time of day. However, a few time-specific expressions exist:

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning | Usage |
|--------|-------------|---------|-------|
| 좋은 아침이에요 | joeun achimieyo | Good morning | Becoming more common, still slightly unusual |
| 안녕히 주무셨어요? | annyeonghi jumusyeosseoyo? | Did you sleep well? | Morning greeting to elders/family |
| 좋은 하루 되세요 | joeun haru doeseyo | Have a good day | Parting phrase, not a greeting |
| 수고하셨습니다 | sugohasheosseumnida | Good work / Well done today | End-of-workday greeting to colleagues |

## Saying Goodbye

Since greetings and farewells are connected, here's a quick reference:

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning | When |
|--------|-------------|---------|------|
| 안녕히 가세요 | annyeonghi gaseyo | Goodbye (to someone leaving) | You're staying, they're going |
| 안녕히 계세요 | annyeonghi gyeseyo | Goodbye (to someone staying) | You're leaving, they're staying |
| 안녕 | annyeong | Bye | Casual — close friends |
| 잘 가 | jal ga | Go well | Casual — to someone leaving |
| 잘 있어 | jal isseo | Stay well | Casual — to someone staying |
| 다음에 봐요 | daeume bwayo | See you next time | Polite casual |
| 또 만나요 | tto mannayo | Let's meet again | Polite casual |

**Tip:** The key distinction in formal goodbyes is who's leaving. If they're leaving, use 가세요 (go). If they're staying, use 계세요 (stay). If you're both leaving, either works — most people say 안녕히 가세요 to each other.

## Pronunciation Tips

- **안녕하세요**: Sounds like "an-nyeong-ha-se-yo" — the ㅎ in 하 is soft
- **여보세요**: "yeo-bo-se-yo" — even stress on all syllables
- **안녕**: "an-nyeong" — the double ㄴ produces a clear "nn" sound

## Common Mistakes by Foreigners

1. **Using 여보세요 in person** — This is phone-only. Use 안녕하세요 face-to-face.
2. **Saying 안녕 to a stranger or elder** — Too casual. Default to 안녕하세요.
3. **Mixing up goodbye forms** — Remember: 가세요 for the one going, 계세요 for the one staying.
4. **Adding "good morning/evening"** — Just use 안녕하세요 regardless of time.
