# How to Say Thank You in Korean

The most common way to say thank you in Korean is **감사합니다** (gamsahamnida). But Korean has multiple thank-you expressions, and picking the right one depends on who you're talking to and how formal the situation is.

## The Three Main Ways to Say Thank You

| Korean | Romanization | Formality | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| 감사합니다 | gamsahamnida | Formal | Strangers, elders, workplace, service situations |
| 고마워요 | gomawoyo | Polite casual | Friends you're not super close with, acquaintances |
| 고마워 | gomawo | Casual | Close friends, younger people, kids |

## 감사합니다 (Gamsahamnida) — Formal Thank You

This is the safest and most universally appropriate way to say thank you. Use it with:

- Anyone older than you
- Colleagues, bosses, teachers
- Store clerks, taxi drivers, restaurant staff
- Anyone you've just met

**감사합니다** comes from the Sino-Korean word 감사 (感謝, gratitude) + the formal verb ending -합니다.

### Variations

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| 감사합니다 | gamsahamnida | Thank you (formal) |
| 대단히 감사합니다 | daedanhi gamsahamnida | Thank you very much (very formal) |
| 정말 감사합니다 | jeongmal gamsahamnida | Thank you so much (formal) |
| 감사드립니다 | gamsadeurimnida | I give my thanks (extra formal/humble) |

**감사드립니다** is a step above 감사합니다 in formality. You'll hear it in speeches, formal emails, and business settings.

## 고마워요 (Gomawoyo) — Polite Casual Thank You

This uses the native Korean root 고맙다 (gomapda, to be thankful) with the polite -요 ending. It's warm but still respectful.

Use it with:
- Friends who are around your age
- People you know casually but aren't very close to
- Situations where formal feels too stiff but casual feels too blunt

### Example Situations

| Situation | Korean | English |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| A friend holds the door | 고마워요! | Thanks! |
| Colleague brings you coffee | 고마워요, 잘 마실게요 | Thanks, I'll enjoy it |
| Someone gives you directions | 고마워요, 알려줘서 | Thanks for letting me know |

## 고마워 (Gomawo) — Casual Thank You

Drop the -요 and you get the casual form. Only use this with:

- Close friends of the same age or younger
- Children
- People who have explicitly told you to speak casually (반말, banmal)

> **Warning:** Using 고마워 with someone older than you, your boss, or a stranger is considered rude. When in doubt, stick with 감사합니다.

## Responding to Thank You

When someone thanks you, here's how to respond:

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning | Formality |
|--------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| 아니에요 | anieyo | Not at all / You're welcome | Polite |
| 별말씀을요 | byeolmalsseumeullyo | Don't mention it | Formal |
| 천만에요 | cheonmaneyo | You're welcome (literally "of course") | Formal (less common in speech) |
| 괜찮아요 | gwaenchanayo | It's okay / No problem | Polite casual |
| 아니야 | aniya | Nah / No worries | Casual |

In everyday conversation, **아니에요** is the most natural response. **천만에요** appears in textbooks but sounds stiff in real life — Koreans rarely use it.

## Quick Formality Decision Guide

1. **Don't know the person?** → 감사합니다
2. **Know them but they're older or senior?** → 감사합니다
3. **Friend or peer, relaxed setting?** → 고마워요
4. **Close friend, same age or younger?** → 고마워

## Beyond "Thank You": Expressing Gratitude in Context

Sometimes a standalone "thank you" isn't enough. Here are useful phrases:

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| 도와주셔서 감사합니다 | dowajusyeoseo gamsahamnida | Thank you for helping me |
| 알려주셔서 감사합니다 | allyeojusyeoseo gamsahamnida | Thank you for letting me know |
| 시간 내주셔서 감사합니다 | sigan naejusyeoseo gamsahamnida | Thank you for your time |
| 선물 고마워요 | seonmul gomawoyo | Thanks for the gift |
| 기다려줘서 고마워 | gidaryeojwoseo gomawo | Thanks for waiting |

The pattern is: **[action]해주셔서 감사합니다** for formal, or **[action]해줘서 고마워(요)** for casual.

## Pronunciation Tips

- **감사합니다**: The ㅂ in 합 becomes an "m" sound before ㄴ, so it sounds like "gam-sa-ham-ni-da"
- **고마워요**: Sounds like "go-ma-wo-yo" — the ㅂ in 고맙다 becomes ㅜ (w) when conjugated
- **고마워**: "go-ma-wo"
